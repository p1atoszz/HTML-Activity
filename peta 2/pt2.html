Kare-Kare

Kare-Kare is a type of Filipino stew with a rich and thick peanut sauce. It is a popular dish in the Philippines served during special ocassions.

Other Recipes for Kare-Kare: A Comparison

Other Kare-Kare recipes differ from the traditional version by using ingredients like seafood or crispy pork instead of oxtail, offering a lighter or crunchier twist to the rich, peanut-based dish.

Beef Kare-Kare

The traditional version can be considered beef kare-kare. This mainly pertains to versions that use cuts of beef.

Seafood Kare-Kare

Shrimp, crab, mussels, and squid are seafood. There is a version which incorporates all of these ingredients. It is referred to as seafood kare-kare. Fish can also be used.

Kare-Kare

Kare Kare is a traditional Filipino stew complimented with a thick savory peanut sauce. The commonly used meats for this dish are ox tail, tripe, and pork leg; on some occasions goat and chicken meat are also used.

Course Type: Main Course

Cuisine: traditional Filipino stew

Keyword: Filipino comfort food, Traditional dish

Prep Time: 20 minutes to 40 minutes

Cook Time: 2 to 3 hours

Total Time: 2 hours and 40 minutes to over 4 hours

Serving: traditionally served with a side of bagoong (fermented shrimp paste) and hot, white rice

Calories: one cup of a typical kare-kare can range from about 377 to 463 calories

Ingredients

  • 500 grams oxtail (or beef shank/tripe)
  • Water
  • 1 bundle sitaw (string beans), cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 medium eggplant, sliced
  • 1 banana heart (optional), sliced
  • 1 bundle pechay (bok choy)
  • 1/2 cup ground peanuts or peanut butter
  • 1/4 cup toasted ground rice
  • 1 tablespoon annatto seeds (atsuete) or annatto powder (for color)
  • 5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 small onion, chopped
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Bagoong alamang (fermented shrimp paste), for serving

Instructions

  1. In a large pot, boil the oxtail (or beef cuts) in water until tender, about 1.5 to 2 hours. Skim off any scum that rises to the surface.
  2. Once tender, remove the meat and set aside. Reserve the broth for later use.
  3. In a separate pan, heat a small amount of oil and sauté the garlic and onions until fragrant.
  4. Add the ground peanuts or peanut butter, and mix well with the sautéed ingredients.
  5. Stir in the toasted ground rice and add a bit of the reserved broth to create a thick sauce.
  6. Add the annatto water (or powder dissolved in warm water) to give the dish its signature orange color.
  7. Return the cooked meat to the pan and simmer for 10–15 minutes in the peanut sauce.
  8. Add the vegetables starting with string beans and eggplant. Cook for 3–5 minutes.
  9. Add the pechay and cook for another 2 minutes until all vegetables are tender but not overcooked.
  10. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
